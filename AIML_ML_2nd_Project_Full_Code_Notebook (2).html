Problem Statement¶

Context¶

AllLife Bank is a mid-sized, fast-growing US-based financial institution that offers a range of retail banking services, including savings and checking accounts, fixed deposits, and personal loans. The bank’s business model is centered on building long-term customer relationships, expanding its retail footprint, and growing its loan portfolio to drive sustainable profitability through interest income.

It currently relies on a large base of liability customers (depositors) but faces a significant under-representation of asset customers (borrowers). To drive profitability through interest income, the bank must aggressively expand its loan portfolio by converting existing depositors into personal loan customers.

Last year’s pilot campaign achieved a 9% conversion rate, validating the potential of this strategy. However, to optimize marketing spend and improve efficiency, the retail marketing department requires a more data-driven approach. Enhancing the success ratio of these campaigns is critical for sustainable growth and maximizing customer lifetime value.

Objective¶

The objective is to develop a predictive classification model that identifies patterns and key factors driving personal loan adoption among existing liability customers. By uncovering the demographic and behavioral drivers of loan conversion, the goal is to enable targeted segmentation and more precise marketing interventions that improve campaign conversion rates, optimize marketing spend, and enhance overall profitability through higher-quality loan portfolio growth.

Data Dictionary¶

  • ID: Customer ID
  • Age: Customer’s age in completed years
  • Experience: #years of professional experience
  • Income: Annual income of the customer (in thousand dollars)
  • ZIP Code: Home Address ZIP code.
  • Family: the Family size of the customer
  • CCAvg: Average spending on credit cards per month (in thousand dollars)
  • Education: Education Level. 1: Undergrad; 2: Graduate;3: Advanced/Professional
  • Mortgage: Value of house mortgage if any. (in thousand dollars)
  • Personal_Loan: Did this customer accept the personal loan offered in the last campaign? (0: No, 1: Yes)
  • Securities_Account: Does the customer have securities account with the bank? (0: No, 1: Yes)
  • CD_Account: Does the customer have a certificate of deposit (CD) account with the bank? (0: No, 1: Yes)
  • Online: Do customers use internet banking facilities? (0: No, 1: Yes)
  • CreditCard: Does the customer use a credit card issued by any other Bank (excluding All life Bank)? (0: No, 1: Yes)

from sklearn.model_selection import train_test_split # Explicitly import train_test_split
import pandas as pd # Ensure pandas is imported

# Reload the dataset to ensure 'ID' and 'ZIPCode' are present for dropping
df = pd.read_csv('Loan_Modelling.csv')

# 1. Handling Sanity Check Issues: Negative Experience
# We convert negative values to positive, assuming they were data entry errors
df['Experience'] = df['Experience'].abs()

# 2. Dropping Irrelevant Features
# ID and ZIP Code do not have predictive power for loan conversion
df.drop(columns=['ID', 'ZIPCode'], inplace=True)

# 3. Encoding Categorical Variables
# 'Education' is ordinal (1, 2, 3), but treating it as distinct categories
# can help the model. We'll use One-Hot Encoding for the best results
df = pd.get_dummies(df, columns=['Education'], drop_first=True, prefix='Education')

# 4. Defining Features (X) and Target (y)
# 'Personal_Loan' is what we want to predict
X = df.drop('Personal_Loan', axis=1)
y = df['Personal_Loan']

# 5. Splitting the Data
# We split the data to train the model and then test it on unseen data
X_train, X_test, y_train, y_test = train_test_split(X, y, test_size=0.30, random_state=1, stratify=y)

print("Preprocessing Complete.")
print(f"Training shape: {X_train.shape}")
print(f"Testing shape: {X_test.shape}")

Model Evaluation Criterion¶

Our primary objective is to maximize the conversion of depositors into loan customers. To achieve this, we have identified the following key metrics:

  • Recall (The Priority): This is our most critical metric because we want to identify as many potential loan customers as possible. In banking terms, missing a potential customer (a False Negative) represents a direct loss of future interest income.

  • Precision (The Balance): While growth is the goal, we will also monitor Precision to ensure our marketing resources are used efficiently. We want to avoid targeting too many customers who have no intention of taking a loan, thereby reducing wasted operational costs.

The following implementation initializes and trains our Logistic Regression model, providing the foundation for our predictive strategy.

What Do the Results Tell Us?¶

The model is correct 95% of the time across all customers (both those who took a loan and those who didn't).

The Reality Check: While 95% sounds amazing, remember that about 90% of customers don't take loans anyway. If the model simply guessed "No" for everyone, it would still be 90% accurate. The real value lies in the other two metrics.

  1. Precision (0.83) — The model flags a customer as a potential loan taker, it is right 83% of the time.

Business Meaning: Bank's marketing team won't waste much time. For every 10 calls they make based on this model, about 8 will be to people actually interested in a loan. This keeps marketing costs low and prevents spamming uninterested depositors.

  1. Recall (0.65) — The "Growth Gap" says that the model is only catching 65% of the total people who would actually take a loan.

Business Meaning: The business, or bank successfully finding the "low-hanging fruit," but they are missing 35% of potential loan customers. These people are "slipping through the cracks" and won't be contacted by the marketing team.

Actionable Insights

  • Target the Growth Gap: The baseline model was too conservative, missing 35% of potential loan takers. The Improved Model captures 86% of potential borrowers, significantly reducing the "opportunity cost" of missed interest income.

  • Prioritize High-Value Segments: Focus marketing resources on customers with high Income and CCAvg, specifically those with Graduate (2) or Professional (3) education levels, as these groups exhibit the highest propensity for conversion.

  • Accept Efficiency Trade-offs: While the Improved Model has lower Precision (0.50), the high lifetime value of a loan customer far outweighs the marginal cost of contacting a larger list of potential leads.

  • To maximize growth, the bank should use the Improved Logistic Regression Model (Balanced). While the first model was more accurate overall, this version is better for the bank's expansion goals.

Why this model wins:

  • Captures More Customers: It has a much higher Recall (86%), meaning it catches nearly all potential borrowers that the first model missed.

  • Drives Revenue: By identifying more people likely to take a loan, the bank can aggressively grow its "asset" (borrower) side of the business.

  • Worth the Effort: Even though the bank will call some people who aren't interested (lower Precision), the profit from a single new loan customer is worth the cost of the extra calls.
